Combat in this game is meant to be fluid and flexible. Players are encouraged to simply say what they want to do rather than using the terminology of the game's mechanics.

There is no “turn order” in this game, characters act how they wish when they see fit. However, there are Rounds of combat to keep track of time and moderate how much one character can do in that time frame.

Energy is used to keep track of how much is done in a Round. Each character gets 5 Energy each round. Using 1 Energy takes about 0.5 seconds. Energy is used to perform actions.

Actions

3 Energy = Melee Attack, Run

2 Energy = Ranged Attack, Quick Run, Pick Up Item, Unarmed Attacks

1 Energy = Shift, Stand Up

Melee Attack: Using 3 Energy you attack a target within your Reach with a melee attack. This represents not just a single blow but a series of strikes intended to circumvent the guard of an opponent. (see Melee Attacks)

Ranged Attack: Using 1 Energy you attack a target with a ranged attack (this includes thrown weapons). (see Ranged Attacks)

Load: This action loads a ranged weapon with ammunition. Energy cost varies.

Shift: Using 1 Energy or 2 Agility you move 1m.

Quick Run: Using 2 Energy you run half your Speed (rounded up).

Run: Using 3 Energy you run your Speed.

Sprint: You can turn any Run action into a Sprint action by spending 1 Stamina. You move an additional amount based on your Lower Body Strength (see UBS).

When running or sprinting, whenever you turn sharply by 45 degrees, you sacrifice 1m of movement. This means that reversing the direction of your movement sacrifices 2m of movement. This applies to back-to-back movement actions.

Agile Move: Every time you spend an Energy on a non-movement action, you may move one meter as an Immediate Interrupt by spending 1 Agility. This represents your ability to move slowly while attacking, drawing a weapon, rummaging through a bag, or performing other tasks.

Jump: Jumping costs 1 Stamina. You may Jump as a part of a movement action for only 1 Energy. Otherwise, it takes 2 Energy to Jump. For those who can Jump further than their Speed, additional Energy must be spent to account for the time spent traveling through the air. Your Jump Distance/Reach can be increased by your Lower Body Strength. (see LBS) If you are Sprinting before the jump, you gain +1m to the Jump. When landing from a Jump, you become Unguarded.

Crawl: Using 3 Energy you crawl at half your Speed (round down). While crawling, you are considered Prone (Exposed and Disadvantage on Evasion and Attack Rolls).

Stand Up From Prone: Using 1 Energy, you may stand up. You become Unguarded.

Defend: You physically and mentally prepare for an attack. Using 1 Energy you ready yourself to be attacked. If you were Unguarded, you no longer are. If you were Exposed, you are no longer Exposed. You now win ties on your Defenses. This defense bonus lasts until you make an attack, cast a spell, or move more than your Speed during a Round.

Stow an Item: If the item is a side arm (or otherwise easy to stow), you may stow it as a Swift Action. Otherwise, it requires a Standard Action to stow an item.

Stow/Equip an Item: Usually it takes 2 Energy to stow or equip an item. The DM may rule it takes more or less depending on the situation (Example: If you have a potion in your backpack, it would take longer to retrieve it). If the item is a side arm (or otherwise easy to stow/equip) you may use only 1 Energy.

Pick Up an Item: Using 1 Energy you may pick up an item lying on the ground with each hand but you are Exposed.

Regain Stamina: Using 3 Energy, you may regain 1 Stamina.

Stamina

Stamina is a resource you can use to accomplish actions faster. It is also sometimes required to accomplish strenuous tasks such as Sprinting, Jumping, and Climbing.

By default, player characters can, once per Round, spend 1 Stamina in place of an Energy. This represents them pushing themselves to do something faster, though it tires them quicker.

You regain all your Stamina after a Short Rest.

You may spend 3 Energy to regain 1 Stamina.

At the beginning of each round, you gain Energy based on your Stamina (see table below).

If you are unconscious, you roll a 1d20 each Round until you get a 20, waking up and gaining 1 Stamina.

Dropping to 0 Stamina will immediately result in unconsciousness.

Negative Stamina does not exist. If you are supposed to lose more Stamina than you have, you simply drop to (or stay at) zero.

Whenever a target falls unconscious, their Stamina falls to 0. Upon awaking, they gain 1 Stamina.

Note: If your Stamina changes during a Round, this does not affect the number of Energy you have.

Your Maximum Stamina is determined thus:

Maximum Stamina = Constitution Score + 1/3 level (minimum of 5)

Tip: Keep track of Stamina and Energy using some sort of token, since they can go up and down a lot very quickly.

Agility

Agility is a versatile resource which represents how quick and nimble you are.

Agility = Speed/2

Your Agility can be increased by your Lower Body Strength (see LBS).

Agility is used in the following ways:
◇ Agile Move
◇ Shift (2 Agility)
◇ Changing Combat Styles
◇ Flinging a Disarmed weapon

Rounds

Rounds are used to keep track of time in combat. There are no turns. Players make decisions for what their characters do whenever it makes sense. This free-form combat system makes combat more natural, progress faster, and allows for more creative strategy. A Round is about 5 seconds long.

You cannot start an action in one Round and complete it in another unless the action specifically allows it.

Action Order & Initiative

Any creature with more Energy + Stamina which can be converted into Energy per Round than another has a right to perform an action before them (though they may also wait). This only applies after combat starts, so anyone may initiate combat.

Normally, when an action is performed, it is resolved before anything else happens. This can be circumvented by Initiative and Immediate Interrupts.

Initiative

You may try to perform an action in response to another creature’s action, even if they have the right to go before you. This is done by Rolling Initiative. You compare your Initiative Roll to that of the creature you are reacting to. If your roll is higher, you win the Initiative, and your action happens at the same time as their action—possibly interrupting theirs. (The DM determines how these actions resolve) If the challenger loses the Initiative Roll, play continues normally.

You may only Roll Initiative once per Round. You may decide not to roll Initiative and treat the situation as if you had lost Initiative (possibly saving your Initiative Roll for later). If another situation arises that requires you to Roll Initiative after you have already rolled it during the Round, you automatically fail. (see Initiative in the Skills section)

Note: There may be times when your one Initiative Roll challenges several creatures (such as if they performed actions at the same time) in which case you may win Initiative against some creatures and lose against others.

Immediate Interrupts

Some actions are Immediate Interrupt actions, which means they may be performed at any time, even interrupting other actions. However, Immediate Interrupts cannot interrupt other Immediate Interrupts. If two creatures try to use Immediate Interrupts at the same time, the actions happen simultaneously (The DM determines how these actions resolve).

Incomplete Actions

The Energy (or any resource) used to perform actions is technically spent as the results of the action occur and you can willingly end an action at any time.

In application, this means…

If the target of your melee attack is pulled out of your reach before you could roll to hit them or deal damage, you could willingly end the attack and spend no Energy.

If you perform the Run action and only move 1/3 of your Speed, you would only spend 1 Energy (instead of 3).

If something interrupts your Stand Up From Prone action (you didn’t stand), you don’t spend any Energy.

Waiting to Act

If enough combatants choose to wait to use their Energy, the DM may declare that enough time has passed that the current Round is over and a new Round begins.

Melee Attacks

Melee Attacks cost 3 Energy. Your Attack Value (AV) represents the quality of the attack or how hard it is to evade. Note that it is a static value, as one’s melee attacks are generally pretty consistent.

Attack Value = 15 + MASAB + Weapon Attack Bonus

Once an attack is made, the AV is compared against opponent’s Defense Roll (usually Evasion). If the AV is greater than or equal to the Defense Roll, the attack hits.

Every time you make a Melee Attack you must make a Combat Roll (a 1d20 with nothing added). This roll determines some possible outcomes of an attack, such as critical success, critical failure, and whether or not you hit the target’s armor.

Combat Roll
1d20 Roll Outcome
20 Critical Hit (You hit the target, ignore their armor, and they are Exposed)
1-3 You are Exposed until the end of the next Round
1 Critical Failure (counts as a miss)

If your Combat Roll is greater than or equal to the target’s Armor Coverage, your attack doesn’t hit the target’s armor and all the damage you roll is dealt to them. If your Combat Roll is less than the target’s Armor Coverage, you subtract the target’s Armor Rating from the damage you deal.

Damage = Weapon Damage Roll + MASAB – AR if Combat Roll is less than AC

Your Melee Ability Score Attack Bonus (MASAB) is added to the AV and Damage Rolls of melee weapons in which you are Proficient. This bonus is calculated thus:

MASAB = (Halved Strength Modifier + Halved Dexterity Modifier) / 3

Some Combat Themes or Feats may add another Halved Ability Modifier into this calculation (always inside the parentheses). You may never add the same ability to the equation twice.

Note: A single strike against something that cannot defend (such as an inanimate object or a person who refuses or is unable to defend) do not require as many Energy as they normally would. 1 Energy may be all that is needed to make a hit.

Note: Since Melee Attacks represent a series of strikes, the attacker may choose to make used of any effects that ended during the attack. Ranged Attacks on the other hand are only affected by what effects are in place once the attack lands.

Ranged Attacks

Ranged Attacks cost 1 Energy. You make Ranged Attacks using a Hit Roll (which is a 1d20 with nothing added) that must greater than or equal to the Miss Chance to hit. The Miss Chance is affected by how difficult the target is to hit as well as how accurate you are with your weapon.

Miss Chance = Miss Chance Modifier + Target’s Ranged Evasion Roll + Situational Modifiers

Miss Chance Modifier = 12 – Weapon Accuracy – Halved Dexterity Modifier

The Miss Chance Modifier is a relatively static number which you add to the Miss Chance of every Ranged Attack you make. The target’s Ranged Evasion Roll and Situational Modifiers are what will change the likelihood of you hitting from shot to shot.

Ranged Evasion Roll

If the target of a ranged attack is aware of the incoming attack and able to actively avoid it they may make a Ranged Evasion Roll which is added to the Miss Chance.

Ranged Evasion Roll = 1d4* Halved Dexterity Modifier + Shield bonuses – Armor Penalty

*This is a Defense Roll. (see Defenses)

Hit Roll

A Hit Roll, like a Combat Roll, is a 1d20 roll with nothing added to it.

Hit Roll
1d20 Roll Outcome
20 Critical Hit (You hit the target, ignore their armor, and they are Exposed)
1-3 You are Exposed until the end of the next Round
1 Critical Failure (counts as a miss)

If you hit the target, you then determine whether you hit their armor. If your Hit Roll is greater than or equal to the target's Ranged AC, then you hit their armor and must subtract its AB from the damage you deal.

Damage = Weapon Damage Roll + Extra Damage from Draw Weight – AR if Hit Roll is less than AC

Reading Range Values

Range values come in a trio. Example: (5/30/120)

Each number is measured in meters. If the target of your Ranged Attack is within the first number of meters away, you have Advantage on the Hit Roll (in this case 5m away or less). If the target of your Attack is further away than the 2nd number of meters, you have Disadvantage on the Attack Roll (in this case 31m away or more). The final number is the maximum range of the weapon in which you can properly aim (in this case 120m).

Draw Weight

Your upper body strength allows you to use and control a greater draw weight. The higher the draw weight of the weapon, the more range and power it has. Thus, you want to choose the highest draw weight you can comfortably manage for your weapon.

If you increase your Upper Body Strength and want to increase the draw weight of your weapon, you will either have to buy a new weapon, find someone who can adjust the draw weight (much cheaper than a new weapon), or attempt to adjust the weapon yourself, perhaps using Craft(bow making).

CROSSBOWS: Most crossbows have a stirrup that the user can put their foot through to draw with both hands, allowing for a greater draw weight than other bows. Therefore, any two-handed crossbow adds +5 to Upper Body Strength when determining Draw Weight.

Aura

Aura is a protective force that emanates from the life-force of creatures. Sentient creatures tend to have the strongest Auras. Most people live their lives without much thought about their Aura, but others spend time learning to strengthen and control it. Masters of these techniques can increase and decrease their Aura level and some can deactivate it completely. A creature’s Aura remains when it is asleep or unconscious.

Taking damage reduces the strength of your Aura, and once your Aura’s strength is reduced to 0, your body is affected normally by any harmful effects.

Your Aura Strength is affected by all your mental abilities as well as your physical strength and endurance:

Aura Strength = 20 + (Aura Modifier * (1 + 1/3))

Your Aura Modifier is used to calculate your Aura Strength and how much Aura is restored from using a Recovery Surge:

Aura Modifier = Ability Modifier of every Ability except Dexterity

This formula is re-calculated every level, so if you increase your Strength Score after level one, that will increase your Aura.

If your Aura is depleted, it will recover over time. When Resting, you can spend a Recovery Surge every 5 minutes. Some spells and abilities allow you to spend a Recovery Surge in combat. The number of Recovery Surges you have is calculated thus:

# of Recovery Surges = 5 + Halved Constitution Modifier

When you spend a Recovery Surge, you regain Aura equal to your Recovery Value:

Recovery Value = Aura Modifier (min 1)

Defenses

Characters have four primary Defenses: Evasion, Fortitude, and Will.

*On any of these Defense Rolls, if you roll the highest number on the die, reroll it and add the result to what was already rolled. If the second roll is also the highest number, you may reroll again and add the result. You may continue as long as the highest number on the die is rolled. Advantage and Disadvantage applies every time you would normally roll the die once.

Note: Fortitude and Will Defense Rolls are sometimes called Saves or Saving Throws.

Evasion

The ability to evade an attack. This could represent you ducking out of the way, deflecting with your shield, or intercepting blows with your weapon.

Evasion Roll = 1d10* + Evasion

Evasion = Inherent Evasion + 1/3 Level + Halved Dexterity Modifier + Shield & Weapon bonuses – Armor Penalty

Inherent Evasion represents the base natural evasion creatures have. This is based off the creature’s Race and Size (see the Size Chart). This value is 5 for Medium creatures and 6 for Small creatures.

When defending against a Ranged Attack, you do not get to use your Weapon Bonuses for your Evasion Roll (plus note the roll uses a 1d4 instead of a 1d10 as ranged attacks are more difficult to avoid than melee attacks):

Ranged Evasion Roll = 1d4* + Ranged Evasion

Ranged Evasion = Halved Dexterity Modifier + Shield bonuses – Armor Penalty

Sometimes, such as when you are facing away from an attack, neither your Weapons or Shields will help you evade an attack:

Unarmed Evasion Roll = 1d10* + Unarmed Evasion

Unarmed Evasion = Inherent Evasion + 1/3 Level + Halved Dexterity Modifier – Armor Penalty

When an attack targets Evasion, you may hit the target’s armor, reducing the damage the target takes. Attacks which target Fortitude or Will are not affected by the target’s armor.

Note: Defense rolls cannot be made under certain circumstances. For instance, if a target is asleep, stunned, or restrained they cannot roll Evasion, and any attacks against them automatically hit.

Note: If an attack doesn’t specify a Defense to target, assume it is Evasion.

Fortitude

The ability to endure some negative physical effects. (Eg: Fortitude Saves are often made to defend against Combat Maneuvers)

Fortitude Save = 1d10* + Fortitude

Fortitude = Inherent Fortitude + 1/3 Level + Halved Constitution Modifier + Halved Strength Modifier

Inherent Fortitude represents the base natural fortitude creatures have. This starts at 5, but can be modified depending on the creature’s Race.

Will

The determination and willpower which protects against negative mental effects such as mind-controlling and revealing information due to torture.

Will Save = 1d10* + Will

Will = Inherent Will + 1/3 Level + Halved Charisma Modifier + Halved Wisdom Modifier

Inherent Will represents the base natural willpower creatures have. This starts at 5, but can be modified depending on the creature’s Race.

Chance Roll

This is a roll of pure luck. It is a 1d20 roll. By default, a 1-9 is a fail, and a 10-20 is a success. This may also be referred to as a Chance Save.

Weaknesses and Resistances

If you have Resistance to a certain type of damage, then you take half damage (rounding down).

If you have a Weakness to a certain type of damage, then you take double damage.

Weakness and Resistance cancel each other out just like Advantage and Disadvantage.

Critical Hits

When you roll a natural 20 on a Combat Roll or Hit Roll, you score a Critical Hit. This Crit Range can be expanded by increased Precision. For example, a scimitar which has Precise 1 will Crit on a Combat Roll of 19 or 20.

When you score a Critical Hit, you hit the target regardless of their Defense Roll, ignore their armor regardless of its Coverage, and Expose the target.

Optional Rule: Critical Successes and Failures

When you roll a natural 20 (“natural” means nothing is added to the roll) on a 1d20 Roll it is a Critical Success. This means there was a magnificent outcome to whatever you tried to do. This does not necessarily mean you succeeded (if you try to jump to the moon and roll a natural 20, you obviously don’t succeed, but you may jump higher than you ever have before). It is up to a DM to determine any special benefits from a Critical Success.

When you roll a natural 1 on a 1d20 roll it is a Critical Failure. This means you fail spectacularly at whatever you tried to do. Again, it is up to a DM to determine if there are any special penalties from a Critical Failure.

Facing

You are always facing half the area around you and not facing the other half. On a hex grid, you face the 3 hexes in front of you (not facing the 3 behind you). On a square grid, you face the 3 squares in front of you and the half of the 2 beside you–when the square is taken as a whole you are counted as facing it (not facing the 3 behind you).

If you are not facing a creature:
◇ You have Disadvantage on Evasion Rolls to defend against attacks from them, and you use your Weaponless Evasion.
◇ Creatures can hide from you (they cannot hide from you in front of you without cover/camouflage).
◇ You (obviously) can’t make attacks against the creature.

Using a Grid

Grids are very helpful for visualizing combat and aiding in the shared perception of unfolding events.

When using a grid, each space is typically 1m.

The rules may occasionally refer to a Space, which is simply a 1m wide area.

Size

Creatures are divided up into Size Categories which give a general idea of the capabilities that a typical creature that size possesses. The Size Chart below should only be used as a guide as the capabilities of creatures of the same size varies greatly. For instance, a Medium sized human who is only 140cm (4’7’’) would certainly not be able to jump 3m (9’10’’) unless they were very strong.

Players use the Size Chart to determine their character’s Inherent Evasion and Miss Chance Modifier. They may also use it to help determine Jump Reach and Jump Distance for their character. Speed is determined by Race.

Exposed

When you are Exposed, you are vulnerable to certain types of attacks, such as Combat Maneuvers (see Combat Maneuvers).

When you are Exposed, the Exposure lasts until the end of the next Round. You may end Exposure early by taking the Defend Action.

You are always Exposed when you Roll a Critical Fail (natural 1) on a Combat Roll or Hit Roll.

Unguarded & Swift Attacks

When you are Unguarded you are vulnerable to being attacked. This means anyone can make Swift Attack against you. You are also Exposed while you are Unguarded.

A Swift Attack is an Immediate Interrupt and if the attack is a Melee Attack, it only takes 1 Energy to perform.

You may only make one Swift Attack per Round.

Being Unguarded lasts until the end of the Round. You may end being Unguarded early by taking the Defend Action.

What makes you Unguarded:
◇ Standing up from Prone.
◇ Jumping.
◇ Being Pushed or failing a Push Combat Maneuver.
◇ Performing a Combat Maneuver when the target is not Exposed.

Surprise

If an attack you make against a creature is completely unexpected, they are Surprised by your attack.

When you Surprise an opponent, they are Unguarded and have Disadvantage on any Evasion Rolls required by the attack that surprised them.

If a creature is wary of an attack, they are not Surprised when an attack comes (even if it is an arrow from somewhere they cannot see). If a creature believes you to be a friend and has let their guard down, you may Surprise them by attacking (however if you must draw a weapon before the attack, they may become guarded during that time, and not be Surprised at the moment of attack).

Note: Under certain circumstances, an attack may always hit. If a target is too shocked or surprised to react at all, and the attack is at point blank range, the DM may automatically grant a hit by not allowing the target to roll Evasion. This is similar to what happens when a target is asleep. The target is considered Surprised, and is automatically hit because they are unable to roll Evasion.

Note: Most people while living their everyday lives with no reason to fear attack are mechanically Unguarded. However, as soon as someone is on guard (becomes cautious) they are no longer Unguarded or Exposed.

Non-Lethal Damage

If you want to incapacitate a target or knock it unconscious without killing it, then you may attempt to deal Non-Lethal damage. Non-Lethal damage reduces Aura Strength just like lethal (normal) damage, but if your target’s Aura is 0, then it will not kill the target.

If you are dealt Non-Lethal damage when you have 0 Aura, then you don’t make a Death Roll, but instead you suffer a painful wound or are winded. You are Exhausted (may not use any Stamina and only gain 3 Energy per Round) until the end of the next Round.

When you deal Non-Lethal damage to a creature which has 0 Aura, you may choose to give it a major injury (like breaking an arm) which means the target is Exhausted until their next Short Rest. You may also (along with injuring the creature or not) try to knock the creature unconscious, and are successful on a Chance Roll of 15 or higher.

Unarmed attacks may freely deal Non-Lethal damage. Other attacks require a Chance roll to determine if the damage is lethal or Non-Lethal.

Dealing Non-Lethal Damage
Damage source Chance Roll Outcome Necessary for Non-Lethal Damage
Blunt melee weapon 5-20
Other melee weapon 10-20
Ranged/thrown weapon 15-20

You may increase the range of numbers you deal Non-Lethal damage on by 5 by giving yourself Disadvantage on your Combat Roll or Hit Roll.

Martial Proficiency Level

Martial Proficiency Level (MPL) represents a character’s martial ability–their general skill in the field of battle and their use of weapons and armor.

Each weapon, shield, and armor set has a certain Martial Proficiency Requirement (MPR) required to use it effectively. If you meet or exceed this value with your MPL you incur no penalties while using the weapon.

If your MPL is lower than the MPR for a Melee Weapon you are using, you deal half damage with the weapon and have -1 Evasion and -1 AV with the weapon.

If your MPL is lower than the MPR for a Ranged Weapon you are using, you deal half damage with the weapon and must spend an additional Energy on your Ranged Attacks with the weapon (it’s harder for you to draw and aim).

If your MPL is lower than the MPR for an Armor Set or Shield you are using, you get 1 fewer Energy each round. (This penalty stacks, meaning if you have both a shield and an armor set which have a higher MPR than your MPL, you will get 2 fewer Energy each round)

For every level of Martial Proficiency you have, you gain +1 UPS, +1 LBS, or +1 Initiative.

You get a Combat Style for having a MPL of at least 3 and another for having a MPL of at least 6.

Improvised Weapons

An improvised weapon could be anything: a rock, a stool, a ladder, a fork, etc. Because of the variability of improvised weapons, it is up to the DM to determine their stats as they are used.

The DM should consider giving bonuses if the Improvised Weapon is similar to a weapon the user is proficient in.

Unarmed Attacks

Unarmed attacks can be punches, slaps, kicks, head-butts, and any other attack with your body.

You may make Unarmed attacks with hands that are wielding weapons.

Unarmed Attacks count as Melee Attacks with Reach0 but they require only 2 Energy (instead of 3).

You may willingly deal Non-Lethal damage with Unarmed Attacks.

Unarmed AV = 15 + MASAB

Damage = MASAB (min 1) – AR if Combat Roll is less than AC

Combat Maneuvers

A Combat Maneuver is a type of Melee Attack but does not deal damage to the target.

If you perform a Combat Maneuver against a target that is not Exposed, you become Unguarded and the target has Advantage on their first Defense Roll of the Combat Maneuver.

Trip/Push Down

You make a Melee Attack against an opponent with -5 AV and deal no damage. If the attack hits, the target makes a Fortitude Save which must be higher than your AV (without the -5) to succeed. If the Saving Throw fails the target is knocked Prone.

If the target is moving during the attack, it has a Disadvantage on the Fortitude saving throw.

The target cannot more than one Size Category larger than you.

Disarm

You make a Melee Attack against an opponent which is facing you with -5 AV and deal no damage. If the attack hits, the target makes a Fortitude Save which must be higher than your AV (without the -5) to succeed. If it fails, the target drops its weapon in its Space. If the weapon is not Heavy, you may also spend 1 Agility to fling the weapon up to 2m away from the target.

Light weapons may not Disarm Heavy weapons.

Hold

You make an Unarmed Attack against an opponent with -5 AV and deal no damage. If the attack hits, then the target is Held and you are Holding them (Conditions below).

The target cannot be a larger Size Category than you.

Held: You are restrained by whatever is holding you. You may make no Movement or Attack Actions and are Exposed. You may attempt to escape the hold by using 3 Energy and making a Fortitude Save, LBS Check, or UBS Check, contested the UBS Check or Fortitude Save of the holder (or contested by a DC if there is no holder).

Note: When you are Held, you are not fully restrained so you may still somewhat defend yourself from attacks. Anyone trying to attack you also has to try to avoid the thing holding you, which could be difficult if it’s another creature and you are both thrashing and grappling.

Holding: You may not make any Attack Actions. You are Slowed.

Push

You make an Unarmed Attack against an opponent with -5 AV and deal no damage. If the attack hits, the target makes a Fortitude Save which must be higher than your AV (without the -5) to succeed. If it fails, the target is Unguarded and pushed 1 meter.

If the target succeeds on their Fortitude Save, you become Unguarded.

You may perform this Combat Maneuver as a part of a Sprint Action if you Sprinted from at least 3m away before the attack. In this case, if the target fails their Fortitude Save, you may push them up to 3m.

The target cannot be a larger Size Category than you.

Note: You don’t have to Push a creature directly away from you, but may push them in a Cone (which starts where they are and faces away from you).

Resting

Short Rest

A short rest takes 5-30 minutes (depending on how much needs to be done—healing, getting back abilities, repairing armor, etc). It is time to catch one’s breath, adjust armor, drink some water, and do any other light activity.

Every 5 minutes of a Short Rest:
◇ You regain all your Stamina.
◇ You regain all your Short Rest Spells and Abilities.
◇ You may spend 1 Recovery Surge.

During a Short Rest, if you are Proficient in Heal, you may make a 5 minute Heal Check to attempt to restore health to another creature or yourself (see Heal). You can also spend 15 minutes to fix Broken armor or a shield. (see Broken Armor and Shields).

If you are interrupted in the middle of a Short Rest, you may have completed some things but not others (for instance you may have only regained half your Short Rest Abilities).

Long Rest

A Long Rest usually takes about 8 hours and represents a restful sleep. In order to gain the full benefits of a Long Rest, at least 6 of the 8 hours must be spent sleeping and the other 2 cannot be spent doing anything more strenuous than what is allowed during a Short Rest.

If you complete your Long Rest:
◇ You regain all your Aura.
◇ You regain all your Recovery Surges.
◇ You regain all your Stamina.
◇ You regain all your Short and Long Rest Abilities.

Anything you can do during a Short Rest, you can do during a Long Rest.

If you are interrupted in the middle of a Long Rest, you may have completed some things but not others (for instance you may only have regained half your Recovery Surges).

Death

To be killed, a creature’s Aura must be at 0 strength.

Once your Aura has been reduced to 0, any further damage is applied directly to you. If even a single point of damage gets through your armor, you make a Death Roll.

Death Rolls and Death Saves are both types of Chance Rolls (a 1d20 roll with nothing added to it).

Death Roll
1d20 Roll What Happens Effect
>=20 You are hit, but it is just a scratch or bruise. Nothing. (Lucky you)
15-19 You suffer a painful wound or are winded. You are Exhausted until the end of the next Round.
10-14 You sustain a major injury (incapacitated arm, leg, hand, etc). You are Exhausted until your next Short Rest.
5-9 You suffer a major injury and begin to bleed out. You suffer the effects of rolling a 10-14. You also must make a Death Save at the beginning of every Round until you are Stabilized.
2-4 You sustain a major injury, begin to bleed out, and fall unconscious. You suffer the effects of rolling a 5-9. You also fall unconscious.
<=1 You die. Yeah, you're dead.
Note: Exhausted means you gain 2 fewer Energy each Round, and may not use any Stamina.
Death Save
1d20 Roll Result
>=20 You are Stabilized and longer have to make Death Saves.
5-19 You continue to bleed out, teetering on the edge of life and death.
2-4 You fall unconscious, if not already unconscious.
<=1 You die.
Note: You only ever make one Death Save per Round, even if you roll low on multiple Death Rolls.

Stabilized: If a creature is Stabilized it means they no longer need to make Death Saves every Round. (A creature can be stabilized by Heal Check to Stabilize them or by gaining Aura by any means)

Typical Death Rolls apply to many situations where you are damaged with 0 Aura, but there are some cases in which your death is essentially inevitable. For example, if you tumble over a cliff into a river of magma with 0 Aura. In some of these situations, a Death Roll might still be made, but the situation may require a very high Roll for you to be saved from death.

If a creature is unconscious or completely incapacitated with vital areas exposed and at 0 Aura, you can easily kill it with a sharp weapon (only spending 1 Energy since they are Unguarded).

Conditions

Exposed: You are vulnerable to certain types of attacks. Combat Maneuvers are best attempted against Exposed targets. (see Exposed)

Shaken: You may not spend Stamina. You also have a chance to drop items while equipping them, stowing them, or loading ammunition (Chance Save with a DC: 5).

Exhausted: You gain 2 fewer Energy each Round, and may not use any Stamina.

Dazed: You are unable to think or move properly. You have Disadvantage on all your Defense Rolls, Attack Rolls, and Skill Checks. You are Exposed.

Blinded: You cannot see, or cannot see well. You have Disadvantage on all Attack Rolls, Evasion Rolls, and Skill Checks relying on sight. You are Unguarded.

Paralyzed: Your joints stiffen and you can barely stay standing. You may not spend Energy. You are Unguarded. You cannot defend yourself against physical attacks.

Slowed: Your Speed is halved (the Speed left is rounded down) and you may not use Agility.

Prone: You have Disadvantage on your Evasion and Attack Rolls and you are Exposed. You may only move as a Crawl.

Surprised: You have Disadvantage on your Evasion Rolls. You are Unguarded. (see Surprise)

Unguarded: Creatures may make Swift Attacks against you. You are Exposed.(see Unguarded)

Held: You are restrained by whatever is holding you. You may make no Movement or Attack Actions and are Exposed. You may attempt to escape the hold by using 3 Energy and making a Fortitude Save, LBS Check, or UBS Check, contested the UBS Check or Fortitude Save of the holder (or contested by a DC if there is no holder). (see Combat Maneuvers)

Holding: You may not make any Attack Actions. You are Slowed. (see Combat Maneuvers)
